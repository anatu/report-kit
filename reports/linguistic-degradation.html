Distribution narrowing under synthetic data contamination

Does training a language model on its own previous outputs erode its ability to comprehend genuinely novel constructions — the slang, neologisms, and rhetorical patterns that postdate its training cutoff? A GPT-2 study across five contamination levels and two training depths. The headline: at moderate contamination, what models generate collapses long before what they comprehend does. The asymmetry only breaks when training pressure runs deep enough to overwrite pretrained representations.

The question

Shumailov et al. (2024) showed that recursive training on synthetic data degrades language models — perplexity climbs, generated text collapses to repetition. The mechanism they describe is distributional: each generation samples from the previous model's distribution, the tails get truncated, and the supported region of the distribution shrinks.

My hypothesis was stronger: if the supported distribution narrows, then linguistic innovations that fall outside the narrowing distribution should become harder to comprehend, not just harder to produce. A model trained on enough synthetic data should lose the ability to make sense of slang it wasn't exposed to, ironic constructions that subvert literal meaning, or AAVE syntax it didn't see often enough.

This is a structural prediction, not a scaling one. It would mean post-2023 LLMs trained on web-scraped data already polluted with synthetic text are accumulating a comprehension gap for genuinely novel language — a gap that no amount of additional training on the same corpus can close.

The benchmark

To test this, I built a 51-item benchmark of post-2018 English constructions across five categories: novel slang, creative neologisms, code-switching, rhetorical subversion, and pragmatic inference. Each item is a multiple-choice cloze with a critical span that defines the construction.

  • When she saw the price tag, she said 'that's a whole ___' Answer: mood · novel slang · emphatic reaction expression (2018+)
  • I spent the whole night ___-scrolling through bad news and now I can't sleep Answer: doom · creative neologism · doom-scrolling (2020+)
  • Not me ___ at 3am on a Tuesday thinking about my ex Answer: crying · rhetorical subversion · self-deprecating ironic confession (2019+)
  • I've been ___ that since before it was cool, you're just late Answer: knew · novel slang · AAVE stressed BIN (completive aspect)

Multiple-choice accuracy turned out to be too coarse a metric — GPT-2 124M answers from token-frequency priors and gets ~30–37% across all conditions regardless of contamination. The real signal lives in the model's log-probability assignment to the construction-defining critical span. So the primary metric is the log-prob gap: mean log-probability of standard text minus mean log-probability of the novel critical spans. A widening gap means the model finds novel constructions disproportionately harder than ordinary text.

Setup

GPT-2 124M fine-tuned on WikiText-103 with synthetic contamination injected at 0%, 10%, 25%, 50%, and 100%. "100%" means fine-tuning entirely on text generated by the previous-generation model — the strongest possible distillation pressure. Three generations per arm, evaluated on novelty log-prob gap, validation perplexity, and Distinct-N generation diversity. Two training-depth regimes:

  • V2 (shallow): 500 fine-tuning steps, single seed — a directional signal only, not statistically powered. Probes whether contamination registers at all. (Figures 1–2 and Table 1 are V2.)
  • V4 (deep): 5000 steps across three seeds (42 / 137 / 256), plus an aggressive-LR variant (1e-4 vs 5e-5). Stress-tests whether the pretrained representations can be overwritten.

Finding 1 — Generation diversity collapses early and severely

The first thing to break is the model's output vocabulary. Even 10% synthetic contamination cuts unigram diversity nearly in half by the first contaminated generation. At 100% contamination, the model is generating near-repetitive text by generation 2.

Finding 2 — The generation–comprehension asymmetry

Here's the surprise. At shallow fine-tuning (500 steps), the log-prob gap between standard and novel text widens monotonically with contamination, exactly as the hypothesis predicted. But when you decompose the gap into its two halves — what's happening to standard text vs. what's happening to novel text — the picture changes.

At 10–50% contamination, only standard text moves. The model gets more confident on in-distribution language, while its log-probability for novel constructions stays essentially flat. The gap widens because one side is rising, not because the other side is falling. The model isn't losing competence on novel language — it's gaining specialization on contaminated language and leaving novel constructions untouched.

The dose-response also shows up directly in the raw log-prob gap, and in the partial recovery at generation 2 — likely the model settling into a contaminated equilibrium:

If you only watched output diversity, you'd see catastrophe at 10% contamination. If you only measured comprehension log-probability for novel text, you'd see almost nothing through 50%. The two failure modes have sharply different sensitivity curves.

Finding 3 — Deeper training breaks the buffer

The natural objection to the asymmetry result: maybe 500 fine-tuning steps just isn't enough training pressure to overwrite GPT-2's pretrained representations. The 40GB of WebText the base model saw is acting as a buffer that protects novel-construction comprehension regardless of what the contaminated fine-tune is teaching.

V4 tested this directly: 10x longer fine-tuning (5000 steps) plus an aggressive-LR variant (1e-4 vs the standard 5e-5) at 100% contamination. The buffer breaks. Novel-text log-probability degrades monotonically across generations — a 2.17-nat decline from baseline by gen 2.

A methodological wrinkle: degenerate collapse

The V4 primary line above does something that initially looked like recovery: novel-text log-prob bounces back from −9.32 at gen 1 to −6.13 at gen 2. It isn't recovery. By gen 2 the model's validation perplexity has hit 1413 — it has lost most of its discrimination and now assigns roughly uniform probability to everything, including novel text. Standard text log-prob also shoots up (−5.73 → −4.65), confirming that the model isn't getting better at language — it's getting worse at distinguishing.

Caveat

Log-prob gap metrics become unreliable above PPL ~1400. Apparent recovery at extreme contamination can actually indicate total model failure. Any future work in this area needs a perplexity gate to filter degenerate runs.

A 0%-contamination control at the same aggressive LR confirms that this isn't just overtraining: the control is perfectly stable across all three generations (novel LP = −7.789 at every gen, validation PPL = 179.7). The degradation only happens when training depth and synthetic contamination are both present.

What this means

The original hypothesis was: synthetic contamination should structurally damage a model's ability to comprehend novel language. The result is more layered.

  • Distribution narrowing is real and dose-dependent. Generation diversity collapses dramatically even at 10% contamination. The narrowing-distribution premise of the model-collapse literature is unambiguously confirmed in this setup.
  • At moderate contamination, comprehension is buffered. Pretrained representations protect novel-text processing through 50% contamination at shallow fine-tuning. The model becomes a specialist on contaminated language without losing its grasp on novel constructions. The widening gap is one-sided.
  • At sufficient training pressure, the buffer breaks. Either extreme contamination (100%) or enough training depth (5000 steps + aggressive LR) does damage novel-text comprehension. The strong version of the hypothesis is supported, just narrower in scope than originally framed.
  • Generation diversity is the earliest warning signal. If you're monitoring a model for contamination effects, watch Distinct-N. Comprehension metrics lag.
Practical implication

A contaminated model can look fine on benchmarks while its output distribution is already collapsing. The conventional "is the model still smart?" check — perplexity, multiple-choice accuracy — misses the failure mode that matters most for downstream language quality.

Limitations and next steps

  • The moderate-contamination results are single-seed. Findings 1–2 and Table 1 (V2) come from one seed per condition — directional, not statistically powered. The gen-2 partial recovery in particular could be noise. Only the deep-training result (V4, three seeds) is replicated; extending seed replication to the V2 arms is the first thing to add.
  • Small model, narrow benchmark. GPT-2 124M was the only architecture tested. The 51-item benchmark was hand-built; a larger benchmark with paraphrased variants would test whether findings generalize across instantiations.
  • Three generations. Shumailov-style collapse experiments use up to 10 generations. The contamination effect appears to saturate quickly here, but more generations might reveal slow-onset divergence.
  • Single contamination source. All synthetic data came from the previous-generation model in the same lineage. Mixing in synthetic text from different model families would be a more realistic contamination simulation.
  • Threshold sweep. The clearest open question is where between 50% and 100% novel-text comprehension actually starts to degrade at moderate training depth. A sweep at 60/70/80/90% would map the dose-response curve.
  • Larger models. The pretrained-representation buffer may be much harder to break in models with more parameters. A repeat at GPT-2-medium / large scale would test whether the V4 result transfers.
